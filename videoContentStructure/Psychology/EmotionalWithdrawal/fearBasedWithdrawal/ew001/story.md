# Story — Rewritten with Expert Feedback

A man's fear of rejection causes him to withdraw from intimacy after marriage, leaving his wife confused and heartbroken. But this time, we know who they are.

## Act 1 — The World Before (Make the Audience Fall in Love)

### Opening State — Who They Are
Ethan Morrison had been the kind of man who made love feel like a conversation—gentle, exploratory, full of questions he didn't know how to answer. For seven years, that's what he'd done. Seven years of leaving handwritten notes in places he knew she'd find them: tucked inside her sketchbook, folded into the pocket of her winter coat, pressed between the pages of a cookbook she'd never opened.

She still kept one in her drawer. The last one he'd written—three months ago. She hadn't told him she had it. He didn't know she kept it at all.

Claire Morrison was a graphic designer who saw the world in color palettes and negative space. She'd fallen for Ethan because he noticed things—the way she took her coffee, the song that made her pause mid-step, the exact moment her smile faded when she was tired. She'd married him knowing he was cautious, that he watched before he leapt. She didn't mind. She'd always been willing to reach first.

Their early days were ordinary in the way that matters: morning coffee shared from his mug because hers was "in the dishwasher" (it wasn't), dancing for five seconds in the kitchen while dinner burned, laughing over something silly at the grocery store at 10pm on a Tuesday. They were two people who fit together—not perfectly, but in the way that counts.

### The First Crack — Cause and Effect Begins
It started with a promotion. Ethan's practice got a major client—someone he'd worked toward for two years. The work was everything he wanted: meaningful, challenging, validating. But it also meant late nights. Three nights in a row, he came home after she'd fallen asleep.

### The Emotional Hinge — Where It Started
This is the moment that changed everything, though neither of them knew it at the time.

It was a Thursday evening. Ethan had just finished presenting his work to a client who praised his analysis. On the drive home, he felt something he hadn't in months: proud. Not anxious. Not performing. Just... capable.

When he got home, Claire was on the couch with her laptop, working late. She looked up when he entered. "How was your day?" she asked, already turning back to her screen.

He stood in the doorway for a moment, the words building behind his teeth. He wanted to tell her about the client's praise. He wanted to say: "I felt good today. I felt like myself."

"I had a good day," he began. Then he stopped. Because she was still looking at her screen. Because saying it out loud when she wasn't really listening made him feel foolish for even trying.

"Good," she said, without looking up. "That's nice."

He went to the kitchen. Made tea. Stood at the counter and watched the steam rise from his mug. That was the hinge: not a rejection, not a fight, not even a wrong thing anyone did. Just a moment where he reached for her with something real—and she wasn't there to receive it. And he decided, without deciding, that maybe next time he'd keep it to himself.

### The First Pullaway — No Villain, Just Misreading
That night, she tried to touch him as they lay in bed. Her hand moved across his chest the way it always had—spontaneous, unthinking, affectionate. He tensed. Pulled away before she could find the warmth of him.

"I'm sorry," he whispered. But his eyes were fixed on the wall behind her.

She meant nothing hurtful by it later that week when she'd said, "I'm exhausted, Ethan. Maybe tomorrow." She was just tired. The client work had been on her mind too—they'd both been carrying things. But he heard: "I'm no longer wanted."

He assumed he'd failed. She assumed he was fine. Neither of them was wrong. Both of them were incomplete.

## Act 2 — Death by a Thousand Paper Cuts (The Invisible Shift)

### The Accumulation
Weeks passed. Nothing dramatic happened. That's the point.

He stopped initiating because the first pullaway had planted a seed: what if trying hurts more than silence? She waited for him to reach because that's what she'd always done—made the first move, created the opening. But waiting is its own kind of withdrawal.

One night, he reached for her hand in bed. His fingers touched hers. She didn't pull away—but she didn't respond either. She was half-asleep, or maybe just too tired to meet him where he was. He felt the difference between her not rejecting him and her not wanting him. In that moment, they were the same thing.

He stopped reaching.

She noticed. But instead of asking, she quietly withdrew her own hand from the space between them. Neither said anything. The silence grew.

### The Midpoint — Her Perspective
Claire started noticing things. He no longer leaned over her shoulder when she worked. He'd stopped leaving notes. She found the last one in his desk drawer—unsigned, unsent, dated two months ago. She didn't know what to do with it. She put it back.

She began wondering: is he unhappy? Is it me? Did I miss something? But asking felt like admitting she'd lost something she hadn't realized was gone. So she waited. She made coffee for both of them in the morning, just in case he wanted it. He drank it standing at the counter, never thanking her, never noticing—or maybe noticing and not knowing what to do with the knowledge that she still cared.

### The Irreversible Moment — The Unforgettable Scene
He reached for her hand in bed one more time. His fingers moved toward hers across the narrow space between them. He paused—just a beat, just five seconds—and she never woke up. He slowly pulled his hand back. Lay on his side. Stared at the ceiling.

No dialogue. No music. Just five seconds of a man choosing fear over love for the last time.

That moment became the emotional identity of everything that followed.

## Act 3 — The Emotional Reveal (Show → Feel → Understand)

### The Realization
Months later, they were sleeping six inches apart. Emotionally, they were miles.

She found the unsigned note in his desk drawer one evening while looking for a document. She read it:

*"I hope I never become someone who makes you feel alone."*

She started crying because that's exactly what had happened. Not dramatically. Not with fighting or betrayal. Just quietly, steadily, the way love sometimes disappears when vulnerability starts feeling more dangerous than distance.

### The Climax — His Admission
Later that night, he sat on the edge of their bed while she got ready for sleep. He wanted to say something. Anything. But the words felt like risks he couldn't afford.

Finally, he said: "I didn't stop wanting you."

He paused. Looked at her. Really looked at her for the first time in months.

"I just became afraid of failing you."

### The Psychological Truth
Fear-based withdrawal doesn't begin when love ends. It begins when vulnerability starts feeling more dangerous than distance.

It doesn't happen in one night. It happens one quiet disappointment at a time—one hand not held, one message not sent, one conversation postponed for months—until the people who used to reach for each other have forgotten how.

### The Ending Emotion
Not reconciliation. Not resolution. Just recognition.

The kind that makes someone watching think: "This could have happened to my parents." Or "This feels uncomfortably close to my own marriage."

No villains. No dramatic betrayals. Just ordinary people making ordinary decisions that slowly create extraordinary emotional distance.

And the quiet truth: he'd spent so long hiding from rejection that he'd forgotten how to love at all. But she'd been there the whole time—waiting, wondering, still making coffee for both of them in the morning, still keeping his last note in her drawer.

The quiet truth was not a lesson or advice. It was simply the recognition that he'd spent so long hiding from rejection that he'd forgotten how to love at all.
