# What I still think is missing

This isn't a criticism so much as identifying the next milestone.

## I still don't feel "the moment everything changed."

Right now I see:

Happy couple → growing distance → conclusion.

What I don't yet see is:

**the emotional hinge.**

Every memorable story has one scene where the audience subconsciously says:

> "That's where it started."

It doesn't need to be dramatic.

In fact, for fear-based withdrawal, it *shouldn't* be.

Imagine one of these moments:

* He reaches for her hand; she's distracted and doesn't notice.
* He initiates intimacy; she's genuinely exhausted and says, "Can we just sleep tonight?"
* He shares a work failure; she immediately jumps into problem-solving instead of listening.
* He tries to explain how he's feeling, then stops halfway and says, "Never mind."

None of these are anyone's fault.

But they become the seed from which the withdrawal grows.

That one scene becomes the emotional anchor for the rest of the film.

---

# One observation about emotional realism

One thing I really like is that your story avoids villains.

That's exactly the right direction.

Fear-based withdrawal is rarely caused by malicious people.

It's usually caused by two decent people slowly misunderstanding each other.

Keep that.

It's far more believable.

---

# My biggest suggestion for the 15-minute version

Don't think in terms of **adding more scenes**.

Think in terms of **adding more relationship**.

For example, before anything goes wrong, spend time showing:

* how they joke with each other,
* their morning rituals,
* inside jokes,
* shared hobbies,
* tiny gestures of affection,
* comfortable silence.

If viewers emotionally buy into the relationship, they'll feel the loss when it changes.

---

# If this became 15 minutes...

I'd roughly allocate it like this:

* **4 minutes** — Build the relationship.
* **5 minutes** — Show the slow, almost invisible drift.
* **3 minutes** — Reveal what's happening inside him (and perhaps briefly inside her).
* **3 minutes** — Deliver the emotional realization and psychological insight.

Notice that almost **60% of the film** is still story.

The psychology enhances the story rather than replacing it.
